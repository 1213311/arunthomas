Collaborative Discussion 1

The reviewed case study describes Rogue Services that advertised its hosting web service as "Cheap, Guaranteed Uptime, No matter what." In this digital era, where people rely primarily on internet-based applications, malicious viruses and spam spread across the web. Web hosting services worldwide take the first step in defending against these spam and malware distribution services in their servers. [1]

However, the Rogue Web hosting providers' dependability guarantee enabled numerous botnets to protect their command-and-control servers from takedown attempts. Rogue providers declined to intervene with these services despite repeated demands from major ISPs and international groups, claiming their "no matter what" promise to its consumers. [1]

Violations by Rogue Services:

Firstly, Rogue providers developed its business globally, although its laws were based in a nation where such hosting operations were not sufficiently prohibited. [1]

Under professional competence and integrity, the BCS code of conduct stipulates that you must ensure that you know and comprehend legislation and comply with it when carrying out your professional obligations. When a rogue provider violated the code, it triggered worldwide pressure from other governments, which failed to compel national-level action. [3] Rogue providers also broke the BCS's Duty of Relevant Authority code, which requires professionals to carry out their duties with care and attention in line with the Relevant Authority's standards while using professional judgment at all times. [3]

As a result, the Rogue provider services enabled harmful malware to be hosted on their servers, causing harm to their customers. This violates ethical code principles 1.1 and 1.2, which stipulate that one should contribute to society and human well-being, recognize that all people are stakeholders in computers, and prevent damage. [2]

It also breaks the BCS code of Public Interest by neglecting to consider public health, privacy, security, and the well-being of others, as well as the environment. According to the BCS code of professional competence and integrity, dishonest, intentional, or negligent conduct or inaction should not cause harm to others, their property, reputation, or employment. The breach in our case study was caused by the company's negligence, which resulted in damage to its users. [3]

According to Ethics code principle 2.8, Professionals in computing should not access the computers, software or data of the other person unless they have a reasonable belief in this or a strong opinion in the public interest. [2] The mere fact that a system is publicly accessible is insufficient to infer authorization. Rogue providers did not consider the public interest, although their ISP was aware that their computers were hosting code that generated infections that were not permitted. [1]

Denial-of-Service attack:

Rogue providers were eventually brought offline forcibly through concerted operations involving numerous security firms and several government entities. This was attained through the use of a targeted worm that propagated throughout Rogue's network. Rogue's computers were successfully taken offline by this denial-of-service (DoS) attack, destroying most of the data kept with the ISP. [1]

Since the malware includes measures to prevent its propagation, no other ISPs reported any damage from it. Spam and botnet traffic decreased dramatically as a result of this move. In addition, fresh ransomware attacks of all types have ceased. However, this assault impacted all of Rogue's clients. [1]

Legalities of DoS attack:

This instance demonstrates a critical aspect of Principle 1.2, according to the worm authors. Given that the worm was created with the express purpose of inflicting harm to Rogue's systems, the creators had an ethical obligation to verify that the damage was justified. [2]

The worm was successful in shutting down online services that were dangerous and malicious. The worm's intention is in line with the moral responsibilities outlined in Principle 1.1. Furthermore, the worm incorporated safeguards that limited it to Rogue's systems, showing an effort to avoid accidental harm. [2] The virus gained unauthorized access to Rogue's systems, deleting data in the process. The objective of addressing harmful software, on the other hand, indicates strong confidence that the service interruption was in the public interest. [4]

The professionalism of computer specialists:

According to BCS, computer professionals should embrace personal responsibility for upholding the profession's reputation and refrain from taking any actions that could jeopardize the profession's reputation. They should participate in the creation, application, and enforcement of professional standards to enhance them. [3]

However, in this case study, Rogue's professionals failed to retain their professionalism with their customers and coworkers. [1]

Conclusion:

The case study clearly shows the harmful consequences of web hosting services' negligence and poor assurances. It explains how a firm that provides services to overseas clients should be aware of and comply with the regulations of the client country. Failure to do so results in social pressure, and laws are also thoroughly discussed.

The worm authors' decision to shut down the Rogue online services was morally justified by citing relevant regulations. It would be preferable if the worm authors could protect the data of the Rogue servers' independent webbased retailers.

Thank you very much for sharing your insights into this case study. I find this case study incredibly ethically complex. As we have observed in the case study provided, we can see that Rogue violated several BCS principles; however, as stated in the case study, they operated from a country that did not legislate or prevent such hosting activities (‘Case: Malware Disruption - ACM Ethics’, 2018).

The BCS code of ethics does provide a fantastic ethical foundation for computing professionals; however, as the owners of Rogue were operating outside of the United Kingdom in a region where their actions were technically legal, I wonder whether they had an altered sense of computing ethics. They seemed to have a self-assigned ethical standpoint regarding their service delivery to their customers.

I find that applying principles from the BCS code of ethics on an entity that does not operate within the constructs of the framework to be dangerous and potentially illegal. In the case study, we see how various organisations and governments justified the creation of a worm to take down the Rogue systems by making ethical justifications within the code of ethics. From my standpoint, I believe that governments and organisations need to take a legal perspective and exhaust all legal options before making ethically justified actions. Finally, I completely agree with you that the worm authors should have considered all users making use of the Rouge platform. Some legitimate businesses would have lost their data and significant parts of their businesses.
